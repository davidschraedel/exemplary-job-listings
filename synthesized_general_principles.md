# General Thoughts on Improving Job Listings

## Answer questions before they can ask them 
(a good user experience means there are no, or are no reasons for, FAQs in the interview).

For example, tell them:
- What they'll do in the first 90 days
- Expectations (culture, performance, day-to-day)
- How you measure success
- Who they work with
- Whether, and possibly when, you’ll get back to them (and follow-through)
 
### Diagram the stakeholders involved in their work, so you don’t have to describe it
*(and put description in the Alt text of the image for accessibility)*
- Inputs to their process
- Outputs and who they serve
- What they are trying to accomplish / what they do
 
### Show some personality:
- Break the norm, and you will stand out as a company. Who really enjoys the hiring process? Everyone is tired of the norm in a tiring process. Like an enjoyable and appropriate joke in a trying time, intentional variety is the spice of the process and will get you noticed by humans.
- Be human: don’t AI-write it all, or at least AI-write it well.
- If you're writing for AI agents/bots, give them something they'll latch onto.
 
### Don’t break the law:
- If you discriminate, stop it (this will make you more attractive as a company and make selling your value much easier). Problem solved.

#### On Posting Ghost Listings:
Don’t post job listings unless you are truly hiring externally. Saves your time, compute, money, and others' time.
- It's a question of ethics. But ultimately, it doesn't seem worth it to post a ghost listing to pretend you are growing, manipulate team morale, or harvest resumes, as these are short-term games that don't pan out in the long run. They will cause your future self more trouble than the supposed help they offer now.
- The only valid exception is strict legal compliance. While these regulations were originally designed to enforce fairness, the modern flood of AI-generated applications often reduces public postings to a somewhat expensive exercise in performative compliance.
 
### Design well:
Break up the wall of text with spacing. It should look pleasant and be concise, from most all angles/perspectives, zoom levels, and platforms/devices (the secret here is to be concise and use spacing appropriately). Design intentionally.
- Treat it like a deliverable to an exec: they don’t have the time. Don’t waste it. You'll get a higher return on investment.
- If you do this, you will likely better know what you’re looking for, and thus candidates will too.
- Don’t be a hypocrite: are you asking for perfect resumes and then publishing chaotic job listings with corporate fluff or too little information? Not a good look.
 
### Write for the future
Make it accessible to AI chatbots, MCP, etc.
 
### Use a simple applicant system
Systems like Greenhouse and BambooHR are a positive experience for users, and less dated in practicality.
- Reputation starts with application experience.
- Antiquated ATS makes you look really antiquated, uncaring/cold/corporate machine, and unintelligent.
 
### Sell your unique value
- What value do you really offer? Why should they partner with you? Why would you want to work with a particular company?

Startups usually can’t offer immense pay.

Corporate giants usually can’t offer amazing growth.
